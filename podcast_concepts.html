Frontier

Every episode opens with a single quantitative puzzle anyone can follow in their head. The founder living inside the answer explains how it got there and where it leads.

Format: 2x/month, 90-150 min. Occasional 4-6 episode arcs. First 20 episodes: puzzles always quantitative.

Approach: Map the infrastructure stack of each frontier. Identify who controls each chokepoint. Those people are the guests, because they have the most unique vantage point on the puzzle.

Season 1: Frontier of Energy
"America's grid was designed for flat demand and predictable supply. Around 2023 that world ended. What breaks?"

16 episodes across 3 acts, bookended by a prologue and an epilogue. Every episode opens with a Frontier Puzzle — a quantitative question the audience can follow on a napkin — and resolves it through the person who lives inside the answer.

Act I · Demand (Eps 1–5): Who needs the power, how much, and why the forecasts broke.  ·  Act II · Supply (Eps 6–12): Where the electrons come from and what each source actually costs.  ·  Act III · The System (Eps 13–16): Who controls the grid, who trades the molecules, and who writes the rules.

Guest tiers: A dream gets (season-defining, hardest to book) · B strong gets (credible, accessible with effort) · C reliable (excellent, readily available). Min-to-air = the lowest tier that still makes the episode worth cutting.

Prologue — The Setup
Ep 0 · "How Much Power Does America Actually Use?"
Min to air: Tier B
PuzzleU.S. electricity demand grew ~0.5%/yr for 20 years (2005–2023), so grid planners built for ~4,200 TWh. In 2024 they doubled their 5-year forecasts overnight. At 3% annual growth, how many GW of new capacity has to come online by 2035 — and how does that compare to everything built in the last two decades combined?
ResolutionFlat → 3% means ~1,200 TWh of additional demand by 2035. That requires ~300 GW of new nameplate capacity on top of ~1,300 GW installed today — roughly every gas and renewable MW added since 2000, built again in a decade. The system was designed for a world that no longer exists.

Act II · Supply — "Where Do the Electrons Come From?"
Ep 6 · "How Much Solar Can You Actually Build in a Year?"
Min to air: Tier B
PuzzleThe U.S. added ~43 GW of solar in 2025. Meeting the new demand curve requires ~86 GW/yr. 2,600 GW sits in interconnection queues — 2x the entire installed grid — but only 20% of projects ever reach operation and median wait is 5 years. Resolution~55 GW/yr, not 86. The 30 GW/yr gap is the entire "where do the electrons come from" problem in one number. The bottleneck isn't panels, land, or labor — it's paperwork, transmission studies, and network upgrade cost allocation. Software-era capital meets 1970s-era queues.

Act III · The System — "Who Controls It All?"
Ep 13 · "How Many People Decide Whether Your Lights Stay On?"
Min to air: Tier B
PuzzlePJM serves 67 million people across 13 states on a nonprofit budget of ~$500M. Its control room runs on 4-second dispatch intervals, balancing ~150 GW of generation against load in real time. How many operators sit at the desks on a typical day, and how many MW-decisions per operator per minute does that imply — especially as the mix shifts from big, predictable plants to intermittent renewables and concentrated data centers?
Resolution~20 operators per shift controlling ~150 GW — roughly 7.5 GW per operator. Each makes hundreds of micro-decisions per hour. The system works because of rules written for stable, dispatchable plants; the rules are breaking as both ends of the grid go volatile simultaneously.

Epilogue
Ep 17 · "What Does America's Energy System Look Like in 2035?"
Min to air: Tier C
PuzzleAdd it all up. Incremental demand by 2035: ~400 TWh data centers, ~500 TWh electrification, ~200 TWh reshoring, ~100 TWh hydrogen = ~1,200 TWh. Realistic buildable supply by 2035: ~300 TWh solar, ~150 TWh gas, ~100 TWh wind, ~150 TWh nuclear life-extension, ~100 TWh storage-enabled — roughly 800 TWh. Who fills the 400 TWh gap, and at what price per MWh?
ResolutionGas fills most of it, at ~$60–100/MWh. Demand response and efficiency take ~100 TWh off the top. The dark horse is existing nuclear extensions. The wild cards are SMRs and fusion, which contribute <50 TWh combined by 2035. The binding constraint, start to finish, is the grid itself — not any single generation source.

Remaining Seasons (Same Stack-First Approach)

Each season below will be built using the same methodology: map the full infrastructure stack, identify who controls each chokepoint, then tier guests by accessibility.

Season 2: Frontier of Defense (Stack Preview)
"What does it cost to defend a country against autonomous warfare in 2035?"

Infrastructure layers to map:

  • Threat layer: Who defines the threat? (Intelligence community, think tanks, war college historians)
  • Platform builders: Who builds the autonomous systems? (Anduril, Shield AI, Saronic, Skydio, Neros)
  • Munitions supply: Who makes the missiles and warheads? (Raytheon/RTX, Lockheed, L3Harris -- but also new entrants like Castelion)
  • Command & control: Who writes the software that coordinates the platforms? (Palantir, Anduril Lattice, Primer AI)
  • Procurement chokepoint: Who approves what gets bought? (DoD acquisition leadership, OUSD R&E, DIU)
  • Industrial base: Who physically manufactures at scale? (Arsenal 1, Neros factory, legacy primes' production lines)
  • Doctrine: Who decides how autonomous weapons are used in combat? (Joint Chiefs, combatant commanders, NATO)
  • Policy: Who writes the export controls and allied sharing rules? (State Dept, NSC, congressional committees)

Unique guests NOT on the podcast circuit: Heidi Shyu (OUSD R&E), Admiral Christopher Grady (fmr Vice Chairman JCS), Mara Karlin (fmr ASD for Strategy), DIU directors, prime defense CEO CTOs (not the CEO but the person who runs the factory floor).

Season 3: Frontier of Biology (Stack Preview)
"How much does it cost to edit a human genome today vs the year the FDA wrote its rules?"

Infrastructure layers to map:

  • Tools layer: Who makes the gene editing tools? (CRISPR Therapeutics, Beam, Mammoth, Intellia)
  • Synthesis layer: Who synthesizes the DNA? (Twist Bioscience, IDT, GenScript -- Twist controls pricing)
  • Sequencing layer: Who reads the genome? (Illumina CEO, PacBio, Oxford Nanopore -- Illumina is 80% market share)
  • Delivery layer: Who delivers the edit to the right cell? (Viral vector companies, LNP manufacturers -- this is the actual bottleneck)
  • Manufacturing: Who makes cell and gene therapies at scale? (Contract manufacturers, autologous vs allogeneic debate)
  • Regulation: Who approves new therapies? (FDA CBER director Peter Marks, EMA equivalents)
  • Payment: Who pays $2M per treatment? (Insurance, CMS, outcomes-based contracts)
  • Ethics: Who decides where the line is? (Bioethics boards, He Jiankui's legacy, germline editing debate)

Unique guests: Peter Marks (FDA CBER director who approves every gene therapy), Francis deSouza (fmr Illumina CEO who controlled sequencing pricing), the head of a viral vector CDMO (the actual bottleneck nobody talks about).
